1. Basic Conventions

Academic writing is not only about grammar. It also demands distance, precision, discipline-specific tone, and consistent source use.

What counts as academic convention?

These conventions help the reader trust the writer: use abbreviations properly, stay objective, cite sources, and avoid informal language.

Define abbreviations once Avoid slang and contractions Avoid first and second person Prefer neutral wording Keep evidence visible

Core rule

Academic style = formal, evidence-led, and controlled. A good paragraph does not sound like conversation or personal diary writing.

2. Sentence Lab

Sentence work is where academic tone becomes visible: voice, structure, hedging, and rhythm affect the reader’s confidence in your argument.

What to control in a sentence

  • Subject-verb-object clarity
  • Active or passive voice, depending on purpose
  • Hedging when the claim must stay cautious
  • Sentence length and rhythm

Useful rule

Academic writing benefits from concise structure, sentence variety, and voice choices that fit the purpose of the text rather than a fixed rule applied everywhere.

4. Paragraphs

A strong paragraph is unified, ordered, and supported by evidence. It should have a clear topic sentence and logical development.

What a paragraph should do

  • Present one main idea.
  • Support that idea with evidence or examples.
  • Use signposting language to show logical flow.
  • Aim for about 45–75 words in many academic contexts.

Signposting language

Use these to guide your reader:

however by contrast moreover for example in conclusion overall because of in particular

Good paragraph logic

Start with a topic sentence, explain the point, add evidence, then connect the evidence back to the argument. A paragraph should answer: what is being said, why it matters, and how it supports the thesis.

5. Critical Inquiry

Critical writing enters the debate. It compares sources, evaluates arguments, and explains why an interpretation matters.

From description to critique

Critical writing does more than list features. It interprets evidence, links it to a claim, and shows why the claim matters in the wider academic debate.

Research questions

A productive research question is usually clear, focused, and analytical rather than merely descriptive. The question should let the writer build an argument rather than summarize a plot or list features.

6. Conciseness and Clarity

Conciseness is not only shorter sentences. It means removing redundancy, choosing precise words, and preserving meaning without clutter.

Writing cleanly

Conciseness or concision means identifying redundancy, unnecessary modifiers, and throat-clearing so the sentence carries only the needed meaning.

Common repairs

  • replace long, weak phrases with direct words
  • reduce expletive constructions
  • convert nominalisations back into verbs
  • remove repetition and filler

7. Referencing

Referencing shows the reader where ideas, quotations, and borrowed concepts come from. It gives credibility and protects academic integrity.

Core parts of referencing

  • In-text citation for the borrowed idea.
  • Reference list for full publication details.
  • Bibliography when your course asks for sources consulted beyond the cited ones.

Quotation tools

Use ellipsis to omit words, square brackets to add clarifying words, and paraphrase when the exact wording is not essential. Do not over-quote complete sentences unless necessary.
