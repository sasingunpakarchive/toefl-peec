How to use this page

Tip: read the theory first, then answer the exercise without looking at the explanation. After checking your answer, read the feedback carefully to build pattern recognition.

Skill 1 — Be sure the sentence has a subject and a verbsingle clause

Theory

A complete English clause needs at least one subject and one main verb. TOEFL often tests this by giving you a sentence fragment that looks almost complete. The most common problem is that the subject is missing, the verb is missing, or the wrong word is mistakenly treated as the subject or verb. The safest approach is to locate the main verb first, then ask who or what performs that action. If no clear subject appears, the sentence is incomplete. If there are two subject-like words or two verb-like words in a short clause, one of them may be extra or may belong to a different structure.

Subject + Verb

Common traps

  • Adverbs pretending to be subjects: yesterday, today, soon
  • Subject already present, but no verb
  • Extra helping verb or extra subject
  • Subject-verb agreement mistakes in singular/plural form
Example: The researchers are studying the data. Subject = researchers; verb = are studying.
Example: Yesterday was very cold. Here “yesterday” is not the subject; the clause still needs an understood subject or a corrected structure.

1. _____ was delayed because of the storm.

2. The new policy _____ approved by the committee.

Skill 2 — Be careful of objects of prepositionsprepositional phrase

Theory

A noun that follows a preposition is the object of that preposition, not the subject of the clause. This is one of the biggest TOEFL traps because nouns inside prepositional phrases often appear close to the verb and can look like subjects. The solution is to identify prepositions first and mentally remove the whole prepositional phrase. What remains should reveal the true subject. Remember that objects of prepositions may be nouns, pronouns, gerunds, or noun clauses.

Preposition + object → prepositional phrase

Common traps

  • Subject hidden before or after long prepositional phrases
  • Plural noun inside a phrase causing agreement confusion
  • Prepositional phrase before the verb
Example: The flowers in the garden are blooming. Subject = flowers; “garden” is object of preposition “in.”
Example: A report from the committee was released. Subject = report; not committee.

1. The quality of these products _____ excellent.

2. The students in the library _____ studying.

Skill 3 — Be careful of appositivesrenaming noun

Theory

An appositive is a noun or noun phrase that renames another noun. It is not another subject. TOEFL loves to place appositives next to the subject so that test-takers accidentally identify the wrong noun as the subject. Appositives are often separated by commas, which helps you spot them. If you remove the appositive phrase and the sentence still makes sense, that phrase is probably extra information rather than the main subject.

Subject, appositive, verb

Common traps

  • Appositive before the subject
  • Appositive between subject and verb
  • Long noun phrase that looks like the subject
Example: My uncle, a skilled mechanic, repaired the car. Subject = uncle; appositive = a skilled mechanic.
Example: A skilled mechanic, my uncle repaired the car. The appositive comes first, but the true subject is still uncle.

1. Maria, the best student in the class, _____ the award.

2. The car, a vintage model, _____ expensive.

Skill 4 — Be careful of present participles-ing form

Theory

Present participles end in -ing and can function in two very different ways. They can be part of the verb when they follow a form of be (is, are, was, were), or they can function as adjectives when they modify a noun. TOEFL often hides the real verb later in the sentence, so the -ing word you first notice is not always the main verb. If another complete verb already exists, the -ing word is probably adjectival rather than verbal.

be + present participle = verb

Common traps

  • Reducing an adjective into a verb by mistake
  • Confusing a participle phrase with the main clause
  • Double verb errors
Example: The children are playing in the yard. Verb = are playing.
Example: The children playing in the yard are my neighbors. Playing is adjectival.

1. The students _____ in the hall are waiting.

2. The teacher _____ the lesson is very experienced.

Skill 5 — Be careful of past participlesed / irregular

Theory

Past participles can be part of a verb phrase with be or have, or they can function as adjectives. This matters because TOEFL frequently places a participle near the front of the sentence to distract you from the real verb later in the clause. A past participle without a helping verb is often adjectival. A participle with a helping verb is verbal. Watch for passive voice and reduced adjective clauses.

be / have + past participle = verb

Common traps

  • Passive voice mistaken for adjective
  • Irregular participles: written, taken, given, known
  • Extra verb after a participial phrase
Example: The package was delivered yesterday. Verb = was delivered.
Example: The package delivered yesterday was damaged. Delivered is adjectival.

1. The documents _____ yesterday are confidential.

2. The package was _____ this morning.

Skill 6 — Use coordinate connectors correctlyand / but / or / so / yet

Theory

Coordinate connectors join two complete clauses. The most common are and, but, or, so, yet. If the sentence has two clauses, each clause must be grammatically complete on its own. A comma usually appears before the coordinate connector when both clauses are independent. TOEFL often removes the connector and asks you to supply the one that best joins the ideas logically.

Clause, coordinate connector clause

Common traps

  • Comma splice without a connector
  • Choosing a subordinate connector when a coordinate one is needed
  • Connector with only one clause on one side
Example: The rain stopped, so we went outside.
Example: She wanted to stay, but she had to leave.

1. The rain stopped, _____ we continued the game.

2. She studied hard, _____ she passed the exam.

Skill 7 — Use adverb time and cause connectors correctlybecause / before / since

Theory

Adverb clause connectors introduce dependent clauses that express time or cause. These clauses are not complete sentences by themselves; they must attach to an independent clause. If the adverb clause comes first, a comma is usually used. If it comes second, no comma is generally needed. The key idea on TOEFL is to make sure the clause has a connector and both clauses remain grammatically complete.

Adverb connector + clause, independent clause

Common traps

  • Missing subject after connector
  • Using a coordinate connector instead of a subordinating one
  • Comma mistake when connector appears second
Example: Because it was late, we left early.
Example: We left early because it was late.

1. _____ the class ended, the students left.

2. We stayed inside _____ it was snowing.

Skill 8 — Use other adverb connectors correctlycontrast / condition / place / manner

Theory

Adverb connectors can signal contrast, condition, place, or manner. These connectors still create dependent clauses, so the sentence must remain structurally balanced. Contrast connectors often appear with a comma when the dependent clause comes first. Condition connectors frequently require a full subject and verb after the connector. Place connectors such as where and wherever often describe location or destination, while manner connectors describe how something happens.

Adverb connector + clause + main clause

Common traps

  • Misusing a contrast word as a coordinate connector
  • Leaving out the subject after where/wherever
  • Using condition connectors with fragment structure
Example: Although she was tired, she kept working.
Example: You can sit wherever you like.

1. _____ he was tired, he continued working.

2. You can go _____ you finish your work.

Skill 9 — Use noun clause connectors correctlythat / what / whether / if

Theory

A noun clause acts like a noun. It can be the subject, object, or object of a preposition. This makes noun clauses extremely important on TOEFL because they often replace a single noun where you might expect a sentence. Connectors such as that, what, when, where, why, how, whether, and if can introduce noun clauses. When a noun clause is the subject, the whole clause may come before the main verb.

Noun clause = noun function

Common traps

  • Inverting subject and verb inside a noun clause
  • Confusing noun clause connectors with question words
  • Forgetting that the entire clause can act as one subject
Example: I know what he said. The whole clause is the object of “know.”
Example: What he said surprised everyone. The clause functions as subject.

1. _____ he said was surprising.

2. I do not know _____ she left.

Skill 10 — Use noun clause connector/subjects correctlywhoever / whatever / whichever

Theory

Some noun clause words function as both connector and subject at the same time. These are especially common in TOEFL because they remove the need for a separate subject after the connector. Words like whoever, whatever, whichever, who, what, and which may serve this dual role depending on the structure. When that happens, the clause does not need another subject immediately after the connector.

Connector + subject in one word

Common traps

  • Adding an unnecessary subject after whoever/whatever
  • Using inverted question order inside a noun clause
  • Forgetting that the clause can be the subject of the main verb
Example: Whoever arrives first will win the prize.
Example: I’ll take whatever is available.

1. _____ caused the accident remains unknown.

2. I will choose _____ is cheaper.

Skill 11 — Use adjective clause connectors correctlyrelative pronouns

Theory

An adjective clause describes a noun. It usually follows the noun it describes and begins with a relative pronoun or relative adverb. On TOEFL, the main issue is recognizing that the clause is modifying a noun and therefore must contain a connector that introduces the description. These clauses are often essential for combining or expanding ideas in a compact sentence.

Noun + adjective clause

Common traps

  • Using a noun clause connector instead of an adjective clause connector
  • Placing the clause too far from the noun it describes
  • Forgetting the clause needs its own subject/verb structure unless reduced
Example: The book that I bought was expensive.
Example: The woman who lives next door is a doctor.

1. The student _____ won the prize is absent.

2. The book _____ I borrowed was interesting.

Skill 12 — Use adjective clause connector/subjects correctlywho / which / that as subject

Theory

In some adjective clauses, the relative pronoun does more than connect the clause: it also acts as the subject of the clause. That means the word after the connector should be a verb, not another subject. This structure is a favorite TOEFL trap because students often insert an unnecessary subject after the relative pronoun. Once you see this pattern, remember that the connector itself supplies the subject slot.

who / which / that + verb

Common traps

  • “who she...”, “which it...”, “that he...” style duplication
  • Using whom where a subject is needed
  • Extra pronoun after the connector
Example: The notebook that is on the table is mine.
Example: A secretary who types fast is useful.

1. The car _____ is parked outside belongs to me.

2. The students _____ study regularly succeed.

Skill 13 — Use reduced adjective clauses correctlyreduction with -ing / past participle

Theory

An adjective clause can often be reduced by removing the relative pronoun and the be-verb. If the clause contains a form of be, reduction usually leaves a participle or adjective. If the clause has no be-verb, the verb changes to the -ing form. This is important for TOEFL because the reduced clause may appear before or after the noun, and the reduced form still modifies the noun in the same way as the full clause.

relative pronoun + be → reduced clause

Common traps

  • Reducing a clause that cannot be reduced
  • Leaving a subject in the reduced clause
  • Using the wrong participle form
Example: The man standing near the door is my uncle.
Example: The letter written last week arrived today.

1. The students _____ in the hall are waiting.

2. The books _____ on the shelf belong to me.

Skill 14 — Use reduced adverb clauses correctlyreducing time / contrast / condition

Theory

Adverb clauses may also be reduced. In reduced form, the subject is removed and the verb is often changed to the -ing form or left as a past participle depending on the structure. Some adverb clauses with be reduce by dropping the subject and be-verb. Others, especially passive forms or certain connectors, require a participial reduction. This is a common advanced TOEFL pattern because the reduced clause may appear at the beginning of the sentence and look like an introductory phrase rather than a clause.

adverb connector + reduced clause

Common traps

  • Using a reduced clause without a logical subject
  • Forgetting that the connector remains
  • Choosing the wrong verb form for the reduction
Example: Although tired, he continued working.
Example: When finished, the report should be submitted immediately.

1. _____ with the report, she left the office.

2. While _____ to school, he listened to music.

Skill 15 — Invert subject and verb with question wordsdirect question vs noun clause

Theory

Question words such as what, when, where, why, how can introduce either direct questions or noun clauses. In direct questions, subject and verb are inverted. In noun clauses, they are not inverted. TOEFL often tests this difference by placing a question word in a sentence where it does not introduce a question. If you are reading a statement rather than asking a question, keep normal subject-verb order.

Direct question: question word + auxiliary + subject

Common traps

  • Inverting after a question word inside a noun clause
  • Forgetting auxiliary support in a question
  • Confusing indirect and direct question order
Example: I do not know where he is.
Example: Where is he?

1. Do you know where _____?

2. She asked why _____ early.

Skill 16 — Invert subject and verb with place expressionshere / there / in the room

Theory

Inversion can happen after place expressions when the sentence begins with a location that is necessary to complete the meaning. Words like here, there, and nowhere can trigger inversion, and prepositional phrases of place can sometimes do the same. This structure is more formal and is often used in descriptive sentences. The key question is whether the opening place expression is required to complete the idea of the sentence.

Place expression + verb + subject

Common traps

  • Using inversion where the sentence is already complete
  • Failing to invert after a required opening place phrase
  • Mixing up there as existential subject vs place adverb
Example: Here are the keys you lost.
Example: On the hill stands an old house.

1. On the table _____ a vase of flowers.

2. Here _____ the documents you requested.

Skill 17 — Invert subject and verb with negative expressionsnever / rarely / scarcely

Theory

Negative or restrictive expressions can trigger inversion. This happens when expressions like never, rarely, seldom, hardly, scarcely, or under no circumstances begin the sentence. The auxiliary verb comes before the subject. This pattern is formal and common in advanced writing and TOEFL items. Recognizing it quickly helps you avoid thinking the sentence is incorrect simply because the word order is unusual.

Negative expression + auxiliary + subject + main verb

Common traps

  • Forgetting the auxiliary
  • Using normal word order after a fronted negative expression
  • Misreading a negative adverb as part of the main predicate
Example: Never have I seen such a thing.
Example: Rarely does he complain.

1. Never _____ such a beautiful view.

2. Rarely _____ late.

Skill 18 — Invert subject and verb in conditionalshad / were / should

Theory

Formal conditional inversion removes if and places the auxiliary before the subject. This creates a concise, elegant structure often used in academic English and TOEFL. Typical patterns are Had I known..., Were he here..., and Should you need help.... The meaning remains conditional, but the order changes. TOEFL sometimes tests whether you can recognize the implied if-clause even when if is absent.

Had / Were / Should + subject + verb

Common traps

  • Leaving out the auxiliary inversion
  • Confusing inverted condition with a question
  • Using the wrong tense after the inverted auxiliary
Example: Had I known, I would have helped.
Example: Were she available, she would join us.

1. _____ known the truth, I would have acted differently.

2. _____ he here, he would support us.

Skill 19 — Invert subject and verb with comparisonsthan / as formal inversion

Theory

Comparative structures sometimes use inversion in formal English, especially after than and in certain parallel constructions. The important lesson for TOEFL is not that inversion is always required, but that comparative clauses can differ from simple statements. When a sentence uses a comparison, check whether the clause has a full subject and verb or whether a formal inversion pattern is being used. This skill is less frequent than others but still appears in advanced structure questions.

Comparative clause + subject/verb pattern

Common traps

  • Misreading comparative structure as a question
  • Using the wrong verb form after comparison
  • Breaking parallelism in comparative clauses
Example: She speaks French better than I do.
Example: The city is larger than it was ten years ago.

1. She speaks French better than _____.

2. The city is larger than _____ ten years ago.
